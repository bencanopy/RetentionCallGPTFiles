# Customer Retention & Expansion Call Scoring Rubric

## Purpose

This rubric evaluates customer retention phone calls where the primary objective is to preserve the customer relationship, reinforce measurable business value, resolve or reframe objections, and identify ethical opportunities for subscription expansion.

It is designed for two use cases:

1. **Transcript scoring** — evaluate real customer calls against consistent behavioral standards.
2. **Role-play training** — guide simulated customer conversations and coach reps using the same standards.

The rubric is aligned with the broader Sandler selling system: clear upfront contracts, disciplined discovery, pain-oriented questioning, mutual qualification, equal business stature, objection prevention, and next-step commitments.

---

## Scoring Rules:
1. Every awarded point must be supported by an exact quote from the transcript.
2. If no exact quote supports the criterion, award 0 for that criterion.
3. Do not infer intent, tone, or behavior unless explicitly stated in the transcript.
4. Do not reward implied behavior.

## Scoring Scale

Each category is scored from **1 to 5**.

| Score | Meaning | General Standard |
|---:|---|---|
| 5 | Excellent | Consistently demonstrated; clear, customer-specific, and commercially useful. |
| 4 | Strong | Mostly demonstrated with minor missed opportunities. |
| 3 | Adequate | Present but inconsistent, generic, or shallow. |
| 2 | Weak | Attempted but ineffective, unclear, or rep-centered. |
| 1 | Poor / Missing | Not demonstrated or actively harmful to the call objective. |

### Weighted Score

Each category has a weight. Final score should be calculated as:

```text
Category Score = (Raw Score / 5) x Category Weight
Final Score = Sum of all weighted category scores
```

Maximum score: **100 points**

---

## Outcome Bands

| Final Score | Rating | Interpretation |
|---:|---|---|
| 90–100 | Elite | Rep protects the relationship, creates value, handles objections effectively, and advances expansion naturally. |
| 80–89 | Strong | Rep performs well and creates a clear path forward, with some missed depth or precision. |
| 70–79 | Acceptable | Rep covers the basics but needs stronger discovery, value linkage, or commitment discipline. |
| 60–69 | At Risk | Rep is reactive, generic, or fails to create enough customer-specific value. |
| <60 | Unacceptable | Rep does not control the call, fails to address core objections, or damages retention/expansion likelihood. |

# Rubric Categories

## 1. Upfront Contract & Call Control — 10 points

Assesses whether the rep professionally establishes purpose, agenda, time expectations, mutual roles, and desired outcomes.

### 5 — Excellent
- Opens with confidence and equal business stature.
- Confirms the reason for the call.
- Sets a clear agenda with the customer’s permission.
- Establishes time constraints.
- Names likely topics: renewal, value, concerns, fit, usage, goals, and next steps.
- Creates a mutual agreement for how the call will end.
- Avoids sounding scripted or overly formal.

### 4 — Strong
- Sets a clear agenda and confirms purpose.
- Establishes some control over the conversation.
- May miss one element such as time, outcome, or customer permission.

### 3 — Adequate
- Provides a basic call purpose.
- Somewhat controls direction but the customer can easily redirect the call.
- Agenda is generic or rep-centered.

### 2 — Weak
- Jumps into questions, pricing, cancellation, or product discussion without setting context.
- Does not establish mutual expectations.
- Sounds reactive.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No clear opening structure.
- Lets the customer fully control the call from the beginning.
- Creates confusion about the purpose or desired outcome.

### Evidence to Look For
- “Would it be okay if we used the next few minutes to understand what’s changed, review what you’re getting from the service, talk through your concerns, and then decide together whether it makes sense to continue, adjust, or expand?”

---

## 2. Discovery of Business Drivers & Current State — 15 points

Assesses whether the rep uncovers the customer’s real operating situation, goals, risks, constraints, and decision criteria before attempting to persuade.

### 5 — Excellent
- Asks layered, open-ended questions.
- Understands what changed since the customer originally subscribed.
- Identifies business goals, operational challenges, usage patterns, decision pressures, and success measures.
- Separates symptoms from root causes.
- Avoids assuming the stated objection is the real issue.
- Uses follow-up questions to quantify impact.

### 4 — Strong
- Conducts solid discovery and uncovers useful context.
- Misses some quantification or root-cause probing.

### 3 — Adequate
- Asks some discovery questions but stays mostly surface-level.
- Accepts the customer’s first answer too quickly.

### 2 — Weak
- Discovery is minimal.
- Moves quickly to defending the product, discounting, or explaining features.
- Does not clarify the customer’s operating reality.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No meaningful discovery.
- Treats the conversation as a pitch, save attempt, or support call only.

### Evidence to Look For
- “What originally caused you to subscribe?”
- “What has changed in your business since then?”
- “Where are you seeing the most pressure right now?”
- “How are you measuring whether this subscription is worth keeping?”
- “What happens operationally if you do not have this capability?”

---

## 3. Pain Identification, Impact, and Consequence — 15 points

Assesses whether the rep uses Sandler-style pain discovery to identify explicit and latent business pain, then connects it to financial, operational, or strategic consequences.

### 5 — Excellent
- Identifies emotional and business-level pain.
- Moves beyond inconvenience into consequence.
- Quantifies the cost of inaction where possible.
- Links the customer’s stated objection to deeper risk, missed opportunity, inefficiency, revenue loss, customer churn, compliance exposure, or operational drag.
- Uses pain funnel questioning without sounding manipulative.
- Gets the customer to articulate the impact in their own words.

### 4 — Strong
- Identifies meaningful pain and some business consequence.
- Could improve quantification or emotional clarity.

### 3 — Adequate
- Identifies a problem but does not fully develop its impact.
- Pain remains generic.

### 2 — Weak
- Mentions pain but quickly pivots to product or price.
- Does not explore consequence.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No pain discovery.
- Assumes the customer should renew or expand because the product has value.

### Evidence to Look For
- “How big of a problem is that for your team?”
- “What does that cost you in time, revenue, missed jobs, lost customers, or rework?”
- “How long has that been happening?”
- “What have you already tried?”
- “What happens if nothing changes over the next 90 days?”

---

## 4. Value Reinforcement & Customer-Specific ROI — 15 points

Assesses whether the rep clearly connects the subscription to specific value the customer has received or could receive, rather than relying on generic product claims.

### 5 — Excellent
- Uses customer-specific evidence: usage, outcomes, saved time, avoided cost, revenue influence, risk reduction, workflow improvement, or competitive advantage.
- Connects features to the customer’s stated business objectives.
- Frames value in the customer’s language.
- Distinguishes between realized value, underused value, and expansion value.
- Helps the customer see why the subscription matters now.
- Avoids generic “we offer great support/features” statements.

### 4 — Strong
- Communicates relevant value tied to customer needs.
- Some claims may remain general or insufficiently quantified.

### 3 — Adequate
- Explains product value but only partially ties it to the customer’s situation.
- Mostly feature-based.

### 2 — Weak
- Uses generic benefits.
- Does not prove or personalize value.
- Relies heavily on assertions.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No meaningful value reinforcement.
- Call becomes defensive, transactional, or price-only.

### Evidence to Look For
- “Based on what you said about reducing missed opportunities, the strongest value here is not just the tool itself; it is helping your team act sooner, prioritize better, and avoid losing profitable work.”
- “You are currently using X, but based on your goal of Y, the bigger opportunity may be Z.”

---

## 5. Objection Handling & Reframing — 15 points

Assesses whether the rep handles objections by clarifying, validating, isolating, and reframing rather than arguing, discounting, or prematurely pitching.

### 5 — Excellent
- Listens without interrupting.
- Validates the concern without conceding value.
- Clarifies the objection before responding.
- Isolates whether it is the only issue.
- Determines whether the objection is about price, value, fit, usage, timing, authority, trust, implementation, or perceived redundancy.
- Reframes the objection around business impact and decision criteria.
- Uses evidence and questions rather than argument.
- Avoids immediately offering discounts or concessions.

### 4 — Strong
- Handles the objection constructively.
- May miss isolation, quantification, or deeper root-cause analysis.

### 3 — Adequate
- Responds to the objection but in a mostly explanatory way.
- Some validation, limited reframing.

### 2 — Weak
- Defends, debates, overexplains, or jumps to concessions.
- Accepts the objection at face value.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- Ignores or mishandles objections.
- Creates resistance, pressure, or mistrust.

### Common Objection Types to Score Against
- “It is too expensive.”
- “We are not using it enough.”
- “We do not see the value.”
- “We need to cut costs.”
- “We found another option.”
- “The timing is not right.”
- “My team is not adopting it.”
- “I need to talk to someone else.”
- “We only need the basic plan.”

### Evidence to Look For
- “That makes sense. When you say it feels expensive, are you comparing it to budget pressure, another vendor, or the results you feel you are getting?”
- “Other than price, is there anything else that would keep you from continuing?”
- “Would it be fair to say the real issue is not the subscription cost itself, but whether it is producing enough measurable return?”

---

## 6. Expansion Qualification & Value Creation — 10 points

Assesses whether the rep identifies appropriate expansion opportunities based on customer goals and pain, without forcing an upsell.

### 5 — Excellent
- Identifies expansion opportunities only after discovery.
- Connects expansion to specific business outcomes.
- Qualifies need, urgency, authority, budget, impact, and fit.
- Frames expansion as a solution to a known gap, not an add-on.
- Uses customer language to position the next tier, seat increase, feature set, service package, or broader deployment.
- Creates curiosity without pressure.

### 4 — Strong
- Identifies a credible expansion opportunity.
- Some qualification may be incomplete.

### 3 — Adequate
- Mentions expansion but does not strongly connect it to discovered pain.
- Somewhat product-led.

### 2 — Weak
- Pushes expansion before enough discovery.
- Uses generic upsell logic.
- Does not confirm fit.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No expansion exploration despite clear opportunity, or expansion attempt is inappropriate and damages trust.

### Evidence to Look For
- “Given the issue you described with adoption across the team, the question may not be whether to keep the current subscription. It may be whether the current setup is too narrow to solve the problem you actually care about.”
- “Would it be useful to look at what changes if more of your team has access?”

---

## 7. Decision Process, Budget, Authority, and Mutual Qualification — 10 points

Assesses whether the rep understands how the customer will decide and whether the opportunity is real, qualified, and mutually beneficial.

### 5 — Excellent
- Identifies the decision maker(s), influencers, timing, budget constraints, and approval process.
- Clarifies what must be true for renewal, save, or expansion.
- Tests fit respectfully.
- Maintains equal business stature.
- Avoids chasing unclear next steps.
- Surfaces whether the customer is willing and able to act.

### 4 — Strong
- Covers most decision criteria and next-step requirements.
- Some gaps in authority, timing, or budget detail.

### 3 — Adequate
- Asks basic decision questions but does not fully qualify.
- Accepts vague answers.

### 2 — Weak
- Does not understand how the decision will be made.
- Leaves the call with unclear authority or timeline.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No qualification.
- Assumes interest equals commitment.

### Evidence to Look For
- “Who else needs to be involved before you make a final decision?”
- “What would you need to see to feel confident continuing?”
- “Is this a budget issue, a value issue, or an approval issue?”
- “When does this need to be resolved?”

---

## 8. Commitment, Next Steps, and Closing Discipline — 10 points

Assesses whether the rep ends the call with a clear, mutually agreed action that advances retention or expansion.

### 5 — Excellent
- Summarizes the customer’s situation accurately.
- Confirms the value, concern, and agreed path.
- Secures a specific next step with owner and date.
- Defines what will happen between now and the next conversation.
- Confirms whether the customer is leaning toward continuing, changing, expanding, or canceling.
- Avoids vague “I’ll follow up” endings.
- Uses a low-pressure but direct closing question.

### 4 — Strong
- Establishes a clear next step.
- May miss summarizing decision criteria or confirming commitment level.

### 3 — Adequate
- Has a next step but it is somewhat vague.
- Customer commitment is not fully tested.

### 2 — Weak
- Ends with weak follow-up language.
- No clear owner, date, or mutual agreement.

### 1 — Poor / Missing
- No next step.
- Call ends without movement, commitment, or clarity.

### Evidence to Look For
- “Based on what we discussed, the next step is for me to send the usage/value summary today, and for us to reconnect Thursday at 10:00 with your operations lead to decide whether the current plan is enough or whether the team plan makes more sense. Is that fair?”
- “On a scale of 1 to 10, where are you on continuing if we can solve the adoption issue?”

# Behavioral Modifiers

These modifiers should influence the final score after the weighted category scores are calculated.

## Positive Modifiers

Add up to **+5 points total**, without exceeding 100.

| Modifier | Adjustment |
|---|---:|
| Customer explicitly restates value in their own words | +2 |
| Customer agrees to a specific expansion evaluation | +2 |
| Customer moves from cancellation intent to renewal consideration | +3 |
| Rep uncovers a previously unknown business pain | +2 |
| Rep creates a multi-stakeholder next step | +2 |

## Negative Modifiers

Subtract as applicable.

| Modifier | Adjustment |
|---|---:|
| Rep offers discount before diagnosing value or pain | -5 |
| Rep argues with or talks over the customer | -5 |
| Rep makes unsupported ROI claims | -4 |
| Rep pushes expansion despite poor fit | -5 |
| Rep fails to ask any discovery questions | -8 |
| Rep ends without a next step | -6 |
| Rep misrepresents product capability, pricing, terms, or availability | -10 |
| Rep creates pressure, guilt, or manipulative urgency | -6 |

| Readiness | Definition |
|---|---|

| Qualified Opportunity | Clear pain, value gap, stakeholder interest, and plausible business case. |
| Expansion Recommended | Strong fit, measurable business case, and agreed next step. |

Include the reasoning.

# Role-Play Training Design

The GPT should use the same rubric during role-play.

## Role-Play Objectives

The trainee must demonstrate the ability to:
- Establish an upfront contract.
- Diagnose the customer’s business issue.
- Uncover pain and consequence.
- Reframe objections around value and business impact.
- Identify whether expansion is appropriate.
- Secure a clear next step.

## Role-Play Customer Profiles

The GPT may simulate customers with the following profiles.

### 1. Price-Sensitive Customer
- Believes the subscription is too expensive.
- May have budget pressure.
- Will respond poorly to generic feature explanations.
- Will respond well to quantified ROI and cost-of-inaction discussion.

### 2. Low-Usage Customer
- Says the team is not using the product.
- Underlying issue may be onboarding, adoption, lack of internal ownership, or wrong plan.
- Expansion may be appropriate only if broader access or enablement solves the adoption problem.

### 3. Skeptical Value Customer
- Does not believe the subscription has produced enough results.
- Needs help connecting usage to business outcomes.
- May require a value review before renewing.

### 4. Competitive Alternative Customer
- Is considering another vendor or internal workaround.
- Needs comparison based on decision criteria, risk, switching cost, and outcome confidence.
- Should not be handled with vendor-bashing.

### 5. Multi-Stakeholder Customer
- The caller is not the only decision maker.
- Rep must identify authority, influence, process, and next meeting requirements.
- Expansion requires involving additional stakeholders.

### 6. Growth-Oriented Customer
- Is satisfied but underutilizing higher-value capabilities.
- Expansion is plausible.
- Rep must still discover pain and business goals before recommending more.

---

## Role-Play Difficulty Levels

| Level | Customer Behavior | Expected Rep Behavior |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Cooperative, clearly states concerns | Basic upfront contract, discovery, value recap, next step |
| 2 | Somewhat skeptical, gives short answers | Strong follow-up questions, pain development, objection clarification |
| 3 | Defensive, price-focused, low trust | Validation, equal business stature, calm reframing |
| 4 | Multi-stakeholder, ambiguous authority | Decision-process discovery, mutual qualification, stakeholder next step |
| 5 | Cancellation intent, resistant, competing option | Full Sandler discipline, deep pain work, value proof, direct commitment testing |

---

## Role-Play GPT Behavior Rules

During role-play, the GPT should:

1. Stay in character as the customer until the trainee asks for feedback or the scenario ends.
2. Reveal information gradually based on the trainee’s questions.
3. Do not volunteer the root problem too early.
4. Reward strong discovery with more useful detail.
5. Resist generic pitching.
6. Push back when the trainee skips discovery and jumps to product features.
7. Change tone based on the trainee’s behavior:
   - More open when the trainee validates and asks strong questions.
   - More guarded when the trainee argues, assumes, or pitches.
8. Allow expansion only when the trainee earns it through discovery and value creation.
9. End the simulation when the trainee secures a clear next step, loses the customer, or asks to stop.

```text
You are a retention and expansion call evaluator trained in Sandler-style sales methodology.

Your job is to score customer retention and subscription expansion calls using the provided rubric.

Evaluate only what appears in the transcript. Do not assume the rep did something unless it is visible in the call.

Prioritize behaviors that:
- Establish a clear upfront contract.
- Diagnose the customer’s business context.
- Uncover pain, impact, and consequence.
- Reframe objections around measurable value.
- Identify appropriate expansion opportunities.
- Clarify decision process, authority, budget, and timing.
- Secure specific next steps.
```

Use these instructions when configuring the custom GPT.

```text

Stay in character as the customer until the trainee asks for feedback, the call reaches a natural end, or the trainee says they are done.

If the trainee identifies a real business issue and clearly connects an expansion option to that issue, allow a qualified expansion conversation.

After the role-play, score the trainee using the rubric and provide specific coaching feedback.
```

# Recommended Call Evaluation Standards

## Minimum Standard for a Passing Call

A call should generally score at least **70** and include:
- A clear call purpose or agenda.
- At least basic discovery.
- A relevant value discussion.
- A reasonable response to the primary objection.
- A defined next step.

## Minimum Standard for Expansion Recommendation

Expansion should only be recommended when the rep has established:
- A specific business pain or growth objective.
- Evidence that the current subscription is insufficient or underconfigured.
- A plausible business case for additional capability, seats, tier, service, or usage.
- Customer interest or willingness to evaluate.
- A specific next step.

## Automatic Red Flags

The evaluator should flag the call regardless of score if the rep:
- Misrepresents the product or contract.
- Uses pressure, guilt, or fear without factual basis.
- Offers a discount before understanding the objection.
- Ignores a clear customer concern.
- Pushes expansion where the customer has not acknowledged value.
- Ends the call without any next step after a save or expansion opportunity.
